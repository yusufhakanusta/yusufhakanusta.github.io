About Me

I currently work in the Cell Matrix Biology and Regenerative Medicine division at the University of Manchester, where I combine biology and software engineering to investigate how stem cells sense and respond to mechanical and chemical cues in the context of aging, musculoskeletal diseases, and cancer.

My research focuses on the integration of multi-omics data, image-based profiling, and computational modelling to understand how cells adapt to their microenvironment. During my PhD, I specialized in mechanobiology and chromatin regulation, exploring how mechanical and biochemical signals converge to shape gene expression and cell fate.

I have developed end-to-end computational pipelines that integrate proteomics, phosphoproteomics, and metabolomics data under varying mechanical and chemical conditions to reveal regulatory networks involved in cellular adaptation. My work leverages machine learning alongside experimental validation (WB, IF, qRT-PCR, colorimetric assays) to model pathway activity, identify phenotypic drivers, and predict molecular responses.

In addition, I have built advanced image analysis pipelines to quantify protein localization and chromatin organization. Broadly, my aim is to develop AI-driven integrative frameworks that bridge multi-omics, microscopy, and molecular biology to advance our understanding of stem cell behavior and cancer progression.
